Case study: redesigning an escape room booking experience

Local shops and practitioners are suffering a decrease in demand for their services and products due to the extreme situation Covid-19 has pushed worldwide. Help a local shop improve their online presence to be more competitive in the evolving market.

Time

8 days

Role

UX/UI Design

Team

Anne Cottereau (UX/UI)
Jacqueline Coriano (UX/UI)
Mouna Ameziane (UX/UI)

The client

Cinévasion is an escape room in Paris near Montparnasse. In addition to their 3 escape rooms, they also offer a private cinema that can be rented for up to 20 people. In our stakeholder interview with co-owner Philippe, we were told that the escape rooms are the core business, but that he felt there was potential to further develop the private cinema. The current website leaves a lot to be desired, and the owners hope to increase conversion.

For our 8-day sprint we would only be delivering a mid-fidelity prototype, focusing on restructuring the information architecture and improving user flow.

The current experience

The welcome page is extremely long with redundant sections like Contact, which has its own page that can be found in the navigation bar.

The pages for the escape rooms themselves leave much to be desired, short on both helpful info and visuals.

The current booking and checkout pages push the info the user needs far too low on the page (below the fold for many monitors), and have sections that feel like they come from a completely different website.

Research

We conducted interviews and ran usability tests of the current site with 5 escape room attendees of various dedication. Here’s what they had to say:

  • “I feel overwhelmed and lost when I see this much information on a website.”
  • “The business should focus more on the escape room—that’s why I’m here!”
  • “When I see a website like this, I close the window.”

We also distributed a survey. From its 132 responses, we learned:

  • 72% have already been to an escape room, and 83% of those who haven’t would like to
  • 83% of those who have been to an escape room book their rooms online

Responding to questions about group movie-watching habits and private cinema offers:

  • 57% go to the cinema less than twice a year
  • only 6% are willing to pay more than 25€ to rent a private cinema

Advocating for the user

Across those interviewed, immersion into a theme was the most important aspect of an escape room. Cinévasion’s movie-inspired rooms were a plus. But the private cinema offer was confusing with a byzantine booking page, and distracted from visitors’ primary objective—to book an escape room.

Even with a better site, though, the offer just wasn’t that tempting. Our interviewees preferred to watch movies with small groups at most, and would typically rather watch them at home. The larger a group gets, the harder it becomes to agree on a film.

We used these findings to craft a user persona that we would refer back to throughout our process. David would be our guiding light as we sought our solution.

Contrary to Philippe’s assumptions, there simply wasn’t much of a market for the private cinema. The interest in the escape rooms themselves, though, were very promising. Our research showed that return rate of escape-room-goers was extremely high, as was interest in escape rooms from those who’d never been.

In order to capitalize on this market opportunity though, we would need to improve the experience of Cinévasion’s site visitors.

Problem statement

Escape room enthusiasts need a way to more efficiently navigate Cinévasion’s website to book an escape room because they currently get overwhelmed and lost within the site.

We believe that streamlining and better structuring the site’s information will lead to better booking rates.

Ideation

Guided by our research, we set the following goals for our redesign.

Home page

  • Simplify the home page, removing redundant elements and making the escape rooms the primary focus.

Navigation

  • Condense the extensive navbar, folding redundant elements into related pages. (The English version of the site wasn’t quite as bad, but the French version could barely fit across my monitor, including two extra options and lengthier wording.)

Escape room pages

  • Add extra visuals to enhance immersion.
  • Add more glanceable, usable info.

Booking

  • The booking buttons from individual room pages should take you directly to booking that room. The Book button in the top navigation can stay as-is, allowing you to book any room.
  • Simplify the booking experience: select a room, select your day and time, select number of players.

Checkout

  • Allow user to pay only a deposit from the website, in addition to the full booking cost.

We started by sketching out some low-fidelity screens.

Iterations

We ran usability tests on our low-fi sketches, which led to our first version of a mid-fi prototype.

More usability tests helped us refine our prototype further.

Checking out the rooms

  • We merged the top image banner and the carousel below, keeping the visuals in one location.
  • We also moved the story details up to just below the images, because visuals are nothing without a story if we want to truly immerse visitors in a cinematic world.
  • Testers loved the floating action button to book a room. However, they were disturbed that the cost to book wasn’t listed anywhere—so we added it right to the button.

Two disparate booking flows

Our booking and checkout flows were an improvement over the original, but users were still getting confused. A large part of this problem was because the reservation process was completely different if you decided to book from the CTA in the navigation bar, or from one of the individual room pages. We had two needlessly distinct booking flows, each with their own issues.

We merged the two booking forms into one and improved upon it.

  • Made the time selector more obviously clickable.
  • Added progressive disclosure to keep the page feeling lighter and therefore not overwhelming the user.
  • The first step on the booking page—selecting a room—is autocompleted for the user if coming from an individual room’s page.

Delivery

These iterations, and especially the updated booking flow, tested extremely well—across both new testers and those we’d interviewed who’d tested the existing site. With some extra polishing and fixes for cohesion, we had our final flow.

We met the goals we’d set from our research by:

  1. Removing unnecessary and redundant info from the home page and placing the escape rooms right up top.
  2. Condensing the navigation bar so that it included only the essentials, with easy-to-understand labels.
  3. Created more immersive and informative pages for the individual escape rooms with additional visuals and glanceable quick info.
  4. Greatly streamlining the booking process with the ability to book a room either directly from the navbar or from the escape room pages.
  5. Giving users the option to pay the full cost upfront or just a deposit while booking to meet the needs of booking for a large group.

Retrospective

With more time, we could move into the high-fidelity stage and apply paint to our mid-fi designs. Further market research, ideation, and desirability tests would allow us to update Cinévasion’s visual branding and apply this to the prototype. From here, we could move on to handoff.

We could also conduct further research and redesign the pages for the private cinema and events.

This project was a great exercise in heavy research of all kinds. When analyzing an existing product, we had to start with heuristics evaluation and usability testing right up front, in addition to user research and market research and positioning.

It was also a great poster child for the importance of User-Centered Design. Our research led us to recognize that there was a gap in business desires and user priorities. The current site was not satisfactorily taking care of visitors’ primary goal—to book an escape room—and interest in the cinema offer was low. We presented the findings to the stakeholders and secured the go-ahead to put the user first.
